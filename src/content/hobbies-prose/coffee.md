---
title: Coffee — The Other Obsession
---

Coffee, like perfume, rewards the obsessive. It's a pursuit where tiny variables — a finer grind, a degree of temperature, an extra ten seconds of bloom — separate a good cup from a memorable one. I've fallen deep into that rabbit hole, and I have no intention of climbing out.

My daily driver is a DeLonghi Magnifica S — a bean-to-cup machine I've refused to leave on autopilot. I dial in the grind size, water temperature, and drawdown time until the shot is exactly what I want that morning. It's an espresso machine that rewards curiosity.

For slower mornings, I turn to the V60. My setup is deliberate: a Timemore Whirly 01S electric grinder for consistent, on-demand grinding, a Timemore Fish Smart kettle for gooseneck-precision pouring at the exact temperature I want, a Timemore Basic 2.0 scale to track flow rate and yield to the second, and a Hario V60 Drip Master paired with a Hario glass server. Every pour is a small ritual — bloom, patience, concentric circles, and the quiet reward of watching a clean, bright cup come together.

I lean toward the third wave — light roasts, single origins, naturally processed beans that arrive with tasting notes that sound almost implausible until you actually taste them. That brightness people sometimes mistake for sourness? That's acidity done right — a lively, structured quality that makes an Ethiopian Yirgacheffe taste of blueberry and jasmine, or a Colombian washed of stone fruit and brown sugar. It's the difference between coffee as commodity and coffee as something worth slowing down for.

The French press is for weekends when I want something slower, fuller, and a little more forgiving of life.

I track every bag I buy — origin, roaster, tasting notes, brew method. It's a habit that started casually and is now well past a hundred cups. The log keeps growing. So does the curiosity.
